## Purpose

This chapter explains productivity metrics from first principles: measures that express how much valuable output an organization generates relative to the resources it consumes. It clarifies the difference between working harder and working more effectively.

## Scope

The chapter covers the definition of productivity, the input-output model, common bases of measurement, a representative catalogue with formulas, a worked example, and the pitfalls of measuring productivity poorly. It focuses on general principles applicable across labour, capital, and time.

## What a Productivity Metric Is

A **productivity metric** is a ratio of output to input. It answers the question: for each unit of resource invested, how much value do we get back? Because it is a ratio, productivity can improve either by increasing output at constant input or by holding output while reducing input.

### The Input-Output Model

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    A[Inputs: labour, capital, time] --> B[Process]
    B --> C[Outputs: units, revenue, value]
    C --> D[Productivity = Output / Input]
    D --> E[Improve: raise output or lower input]
```

## Why Productivity Metrics Matter

Productivity is the foundation of sustainable profitability and competitiveness. Rising productivity means a business can grow output, margins, and wages without proportional cost increases. Because inputs are finite, productivity gains are often the most durable source of advantage. They also reveal whether growth is efficient or merely the result of adding more resources.

## Representative Productivity Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| Labour Productivity | Output / Labour hours | Value produced per hour worked |
| Revenue per Employee | Total revenue / Number of employees | Revenue generated per head |
| Output per Shift | Units produced / Shifts worked | Production efficiency per work period |
| Sales per Representative | Total sales / Number of reps | Selling effectiveness per person |
| Cost per Unit | Total cost / Units produced | Resource efficiency in production |

## Worked Example

A company generates 4,000,000 currency units in annual revenue with 20 employees.

- Revenue per Employee = 4,000,000 / 20 = **200,000 per employee**.

After automating a manual process, the company grows revenue to 5,000,000 with 22 employees: 5,000,000 / 22 = about 227,000 per employee. Output grew faster than headcount, showing a genuine productivity gain rather than growth purchased solely by hiring.

## Pitfalls

Productivity metrics can mislead when output is measured by volume rather than value, encouraging quantity at the expense of quality. Sound practice pairs productivity metrics with quality metrics so that efficiency gains do not erode the value being produced.

## Relevance to WORLD

An AI Business Partner measures productivity across people, capital, and time, distinguishing efficient growth from growth that merely consumes more resources. By automating routine work itself, the platform directly raises output per person, and it advises the founder on where additional automation or reallocation would yield the greatest productivity return.
