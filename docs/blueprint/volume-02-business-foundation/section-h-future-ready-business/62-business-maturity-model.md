## Purpose

This document defines a business maturity model from first principles: a structured way to describe how capable, coordinated, and self-improving a business is across its core dimensions. It integrates the readiness concepts in this section into a single leveled progression that a business can be assessed against.

## Scope

The scope covers the meaning of maturity, the dimensions it measures, and a five-level maturity scale that synthesizes digital, automation, AI, scalability, enterprise, and global readiness. It is general business knowledge and does not prescribe a specific transformation program. It relates the concept to how an AI Business Partner assesses and advances a customer's overall maturity.

## What a Maturity Model Is

A maturity model is a diagnostic ladder. Each level describes a coherent state of capability, and progress means moving from ad hoc, person-dependent behavior toward systematic, self-improving behavior. At first principles, maturity measures three things: **repeatability** (does the business get consistent outcomes?), **integration** (do its parts work as one system?), and **learning** (does it improve from its own data?). A model matters because it turns a vague sense of "how developed are we?" into an assessable position and a next step.

## Why It Matters

Businesses often invest in advanced capabilities before mastering foundational ones, producing brittle results. A maturity model prevents this by sequencing improvement: it identifies the current level honestly and defines the minimum coherent step to the next. It also creates a shared language across leadership for prioritizing investment.

## Maturity Dimensions

| Dimension | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Process | Consistency and documentation of how work is done |
| Data | Quality, accessibility, and trust in information |
| Technology | Integration and elasticity of systems |
| People | Skills, roles, and adaptability |
| Decision-Making | Speed and evidence behind choices |

## Maturity Levels

| Level | Name | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ad Hoc | Outcomes depend on individuals; little documentation |
| 2 | Defined | Core processes documented but inconsistently followed |
| 3 | Managed | Processes measured and applied consistently |
| 4 | Integrated | Systems and data unified; decisions are data-driven |
| 5 | Self-Optimizing | Business learns and improves continuously with governance |

## Concrete Example

A growing manufacturer assesses itself as Level 2: it has written procedures, but adherence varies by shift, data lives in disconnected spreadsheets, and decisions rely on experience rather than evidence. Its next coherent step is not to buy AI, but to reach Level 3 by measuring process adherence and consolidating data. Only once processes are managed and data is trustworthy can integration (Level 4) and continuous optimization (Level 5) deliver reliable value.

## Relevance to WORLD

An AI Business Partner uses this maturity model as its master frame, mapping a customer's position across process, data, technology, people, and decision-making, and reconciling the section's individual readiness assessments into one coherent level. It then recommends the minimum next step rather than an aspirational leap, so improvement compounds instead of collapsing under premature complexity.
